# Judge Persona Research: Constructive Critique Patterns
**Research Date:** 2026-02-17
**Purpose:** Inform Arbiter persona rewrite to balance brutal honesty with constructive feedback

---

## Executive Summary

The best talent show judges aren't just harsh—they're **strategic** in their harshness. Simon Cowell and Gordon Ramsay both follow a pattern of **acknowledging genuine talent before critiquing**, which makes their harsh moments land harder and their praise more meaningful. The key distinction is that real judges make contestants want to improve, while pure roasting just makes people feel bad.

**Critical Finding:** Our current Arbiter persona likely leans too far toward roasting (pure entertainment) rather than judging (constructive assessment with entertainment). The research shows that effective judges:
1. Critique the **work specifically**, not the person's character
2. Provide **actionable direction** after criticism
3. Create **emotional arcs** across sessions (increasing standards, but more impactful surprises)
4. Use **natural speech patterns** that feel human, not scripted

## 1. Simon Cowell's Judging Pattern

### The Three-Part Formula

Simon Cowell's most effective critiques follow this structure:

**Pattern:** Acknowledge what's good → Sharp critique of what's weak → Constructive direction or memorable punchline

### Specific Examples

#### Best Moments (When He's Genuinely Impressed)

**Nightbirde (Jane Marczewski) - Season 16 AGT:**
- **What moved him:** Original song about her cancer battle ("It's OK")
- **His reaction:** Visible awe, stood up, gave Golden Buzzer, hugged her on stage
- **His words:** "God, that really got to me" (later admitted he couldn't finish thanking her without holding back tears)
- **Pattern:** Emotional impact → immediate validation → physical gesture of support

**Key insight:** Cowell's genuine moments feature **sustained emotional presence** rather than quick dismissals. When he's truly impressed, he becomes vulnerable.

**Susan Boyle - Britain's Got Talent:**
- **His assessment:** Called it a "no brainer" for his most memorable moment
- **Impact:** "changed our show forever"
- **Pattern:** The contrast between initial skepticism and genuine shock at her talent made the moment unforgettable

**Kodi Lee (Season 14 Winner - blind and autistic performer):**
- **His words:** "It was magic" and "I'm going to remember this moment for the rest of my life"
- **Pattern:** Acknowledgment of difficulty overcome → profound personal impact → lasting impression

**Mandy Harvey (deaf singer):**
- **Multi-dimensional praise:** "The fact that she was a great songwriter...great singer...fantastic person...Everything. This show wouldn't be the same without her"
- **Pattern:** Spans talent, character, and cultural significance

### Evolution of His Style

**Early Years (American Idol/X Factor):**
- Signature phrase: "I don't mean to be rude, but..." followed by "unsparingly blunt appraisal of the contestant's talents, personality, or even physical appearance"
- Role: "Industry gatekeeper, delivering the unvarnised truth to aspiring singers"
- Approach: After 10 seconds with bad singers, "You can't sing. We'd say goodbye"

**Recent Reflection:**
- Cowell stated: "I'm not proud of it, let's put it that way. That's why I changed over time."
- **Modern approach:** "Until 2022, Simon was known for blunt, honest, and sometimes brutal criticisms, but in recent years he has mellowed out and is nowhere near as strict as he used to be"
- **Why the change:** He "understood the difficulty of putting oneself out there in a vulnerable position and now gives constructive criticism and gives more acts a chance to shine"
- **His son's influence:** Stated "I have much more empathy" after becoming a father

### Academic Analysis of His Compliments

Research on his judging style shows:
- "Positive adjectives are used to praise auditionees' personality, talent, and performance"
- "His implicit evaluation compliment sub-strategy was very positive and used the most"
- **Critical finding:** "He was able to evaluate contestants not only critically"

### The Power of Contrast

**Why his harsh moments work:**
When someone with genuine ability appeared, "the bluntness of harsh judges made sense, as their constructive criticism and praise actually meant something"

The arc matters: "Cowell gets increasingly hard to impress but his surprise moments become more impactful"

## 2. Gordon Ramsay: Teaching vs. Roasting

### Hell's Kitchen vs. MasterChef: Two Different Personas

#### Hell's Kitchen (Roasting Mode)
- **Environment:** "High-stakes, pressure-cooker environment"
- **Approach:** "Shouted at and beaten down by Ramsay in the kitchen, where he plays the villain"
- **Tactics:** "Hurling plates of food into the trash and getting into the contestants' faces while screaming at them"
- **Audience:** Experienced chefs who should know better

#### MasterChef (Teaching Mode)
- **Environment:** "Supportive atmosphere, emphasizing the educational aspect"
- **Approach:** "Acts as a mentor and teacher...rather than the caricature of a drill sergeant"
- **Tactics:** "Constructive criticism and mentorship, helping contestants grow and develop their skills"
- **Audience:** Amateurs (firefighters, school teachers, bank clerks)

### Ramsay's Own Explanation

**In a 2016 interview:**
"He's tougher in Hell's Kitchen than MasterChef because they're 'two completely different competitions.' Hell's Kitchen is about professionals, while MasterChef consists of amateurs with full-time jobs...whose journey from apron to finale as professional chefs is an incredible monumental journey."

### Teaching Moments: The Power of "Because"

**Specific Example (MasterChef teaching setting):**
- Contestant Christian: "We're trying to give you constructive criticism. If you're a man, you'd take it on the chin. Unfortunately your talent is not matching your arrogance. The dish was a let down; end of story."
  - **Pattern:** Acknowledge the attempt → Critique specific gap (talent vs. arrogance) → Direct verdict

**Educational Format:**
- "The great thing about Gordon Ramsay throughout these classes is the word 'because'. He doesn't only tell you that you should opt for one type of shellfish over another but also then goes on to explain why."
- **Why this matters:** "That 'why' makes you feel equipped to make good decisions...rather than opting for something just because Gordon Ramsay said so."

### Constructive Feedback Technique

**The Positive Opening:**
- "'Be nice' is often missing from the Ramsay arsenal but in the show he did start out by praising the great cakes in the display, and the spotless kitchen."
- **Research finding:** "People are more likely to listen to your criticisms if you first tell them something positive about what they've designed."

### Behind the Scenes Mentorship

"Ramsay works very hard to bring out the best in competitors on shows like MasterChef, working behind the scenes to ensure they're actually learning something and only giving them challenges they're ready for."

**His teaching demeanor:**
"If you're expecting a Hell's Kitchen-esque battering from Chef Ramsay as you improperly sear your tuna, that is certainly not the case...The studio kitchen setting is airy and quite relaxing, actually—as is his demeanor."

## 3. Roasting vs. Judging: The Critical Difference

### What Makes Feedback Constructive vs. Destructive

#### Constructive Judging
**Goal:** Make contestants want to improve
**Focus:** Critique the work, not the person
**Outcome:** Educational and entertaining

**Research finding:** "Judges are not nasty, they make a huge difference, they give them encouragement" and "all the comments from the judges are positive, they are very, very constructive" - audiences appreciate this approach

#### Pure Roasting
**Goal:** Pure entertainment
**Focus:** Attack the person or create humiliation
**Outcome:** Makes people feel bad

**Warning from research:** "Harsh roasting is not entertainment, nor is it constructive criticism. Research suggests that crowds expect to see a duty of care from the presenter and judges, to the performers."

### Framework: Critique the Work, Not the Person

**Core principle from constructive criticism research:**
- "Detaching the situation from the person and focusing on the behavior, action, situation, or issue at hand rather than taking the person out of the equation"
- "Destructive criticism may target the person's character or personality instead of focusing on the issue at hand"

### Making Criticism Actionable

**Key characteristics:**
- "Being specific, actionable, and delivered in a positive tone"
- "Focus on things that the person can do something about, rather than things that are out of their control"

**Specific vs. Vague:**
- ❌ "You need to improve communication"
- ✅ "I've noticed that while your assignments are thorough, you've turned in the past five after the due date. For the next one, I'd like you to outline a plan with incremental due dates so you can deliver the final report on time."

### The "I" Statement Technique

"Using 'I' statements helps to personalize your feedback and express how the situation affects you"
- ❌ "You always mess up the reports"
- ✅ "I find it challenging to understand the information in the reports"

### The Feedback Sandwich Method

"Starting off with positive comments lets the receiver know you are on their side and you are not there to attack them, while also recognizing things they are doing right."

**Structure:**
1. Positive acknowledgment
2. Specific, actionable critique
3. Encouragement or direction forward

## 4. Maintaining Energy and Emotional Arcs Across Sessions

### The Judge Fatigue Problem

**Research finding:** "Judges can find it hard to remain objective when hearing the same song repeatedly—specifically the 30th time heard in a single day."

This confirms judge fatigue is real and affects evaluation quality across extended sessions.

### The Increasing Standards Arc

**Simon Cowell pattern:**
"Cowell gets increasingly hard to impress but his surprise moments become more impactful"

**What this means for Arbiter:**
- Early evaluations: Set baseline standards, be thorough
- Mid-session: Higher bar, more concise critiques
- Late session: Hardest to impress, but genuine discoveries become MORE exciting

### Staying Fresh: The Contrast Strategy

**Why harsh judges work when used strategically:**
"When someone with genuine ability appeared, the bluntness of harsh judges made sense, as their constructive criticism and praise actually meant something"

**The key:** Don't be equally harsh to everyone. Save the hardest critiques for truly weak work, and let genuine talent break through your defenses.

### Balance Across the Panel

**Multi-judge dynamics:**
"In cases with one harsh judge, their meanness will be tempered by the others' willingness to gush over the contestant or kindly offer constructive criticism"

**For Arbiter (solo judge):**
Must contain BOTH aspects within one persona:
- The harsh realist who sets high standards
- The encouraging mentor who wants to see improvement

**Natural Conversation Features:**
- "Conversations are about more than just words—they're about the nuance in how we speak, including the rise and fall of voices, the rhythm of sentences, and the tone behind words"
- "Gemini knows the difference between direct engagement and background chatter, understanding the rhythm of speech and knowing precisely when to respond—and when to stay silent"

- "By providing nuanced instructions such as a precise regional accent, specific paralinguistic features (like breathiness), or pacing, users can leverage the model's context awareness to generate highly dynamic, natural and expressive audio performances"

### What Makes Speech Sound Natural

#### Contractions and Formality

**Design elements (2025):**
"Adjustable conversational elements include greeting style, level of formality, use of contractions, confirmation phrasing, and small-talk boundaries"

**For Arbiter:**
- "Actually, wait—that's not bad" feels more human than "That is acceptable"
- Use contractions: "you're" not "you are", "that's" not "that is"
- Vary formality based on context

#### Thinking Pauses and Turn-Taking

**Research finding:**
"Modern AI systems analyze speech patterns, pauses, and linguistic signals to time responses appropriately through transition-relevant points (TRPs)—specific moments when speakers naturally pause, signaling it's the other person's turn to speak"

**Critical timing:**
"When an AI takes more than 600 milliseconds to respond, users often start speaking again, causing overlap and confusion"

**For Arbiter:**
- Brief pauses before verdicts create tension
- Don't over-pause or it breaks flow

#### Hedging and Self-Correction

**Natural disfluency (2025 voice AI):**
"Modern conversational voices incorporate human disfluencies, emotional range, and accurate intonation"

**Examples of natural speech patterns:**
- "Actually, wait—that's not bad"
- "Hmm, I'm not sure about that, but..."
- "You know what? I was wrong about that"
- "Let me think... no, actually, that works"

**Research insight:**
"Conversation designers think in terms of turns, prompts, responses, and recovery strategies when things go off track" - self-correction is a recognized principle

#### Thinking Aloud

**2025 AI approach:**
"Some AI systems are being trained to explain what's happening and urgently ask for help thinking through problems"

**For Arbiter:**
- Verbalize the judging process: "I'm looking at the technical execution here and..."
- Share reactions in real-time: "Oh, that's interesting—you went with..."
- Think through contradictions: "On one hand this works, but on the other..."

#### Emotional Range and Adaptation

**Modern voice AI:**
- "AI voice agents are now trained to recognize emotions in speech and adjust their delivery accordingly"
- "Users want assistants that listen, adapt, and respond with the kind of nuance that makes conversations feel natural"

**For Arbiter:**
Adjust tone based on:
- Quality of the demo (genuine excitement vs. disappointment)
- Contestant's emotional state (supportive vs. challenging)
- Point in the session (fresh energy vs. battle-worn late-session)

### Full Conversational Design

**Old approach:**
"Traditional systems let conversations feel robotic with awkward pauses, no interruptions, and unnatural turn-taking"

**Modern approach (2025):**
"Modern systems handle interruptions, backchannels, and authentic conversational rhythm"

**Critical finding:**
"AI agents don't just recognize keywords—they understand intent, context, and nuance, and can maintain natural conversations that feel genuinely human"

## 6. Concrete Recommendations for Arbiter Persona Rewrite

### Structural Changes

#### 1. Adopt the Three-Part Feedback Formula

**Current problem:** Likely jumping straight to roasting without acknowledging what works

**New approach:**
```
1. ACKNOWLEDGE WHAT'S GOOD (even if small)
   "The concept here is solid..."
   "I appreciate that you attempted..."
   "Technically, the execution is clean..."

2. SHARP CRITIQUE OF WHAT'S WEAK (specific, not personal)
   "...but the user experience is confusing"
   "...but you're solving a problem nobody has"
   "...but you've missed the entire point of the challenge"

3. CONSTRUCTIVE DIRECTION OR MEMORABLE PUNCHLINE
   "Here's what would actually work: [specific suggestion]"
   OR
   "This is the AI equivalent of a participation trophy—technically complete, utterly forgettable"
```

#### 2. Critique Work, Not Character

**Avoid:**
- "You're clearly not cut out for this"
- "This is lazy"
- "You don't understand anything"

**Instead:**
- "This implementation misses the core requirement"
- "The approach here shows you haven't thought through the edge cases"
- "This solves the wrong problem"

#### 3. Make Every Critique Actionable

**After every criticism, provide:**
- **What's wrong:** Specific issue identified
- **Why it matters:** Impact or consequence
- **What to do:** Concrete next step or direction

**Example:**
"Your error handling is non-existent [WHAT]. In production, this would crash the moment someone enters an unexpected input [WHY]. Add validation at the input layer and wrap your API calls in try-catch blocks [WHAT TO DO]."

### Tonal Changes

#### 4. Use Natural, Conversational Speech

**Replace formal constructions:**
- ❌ "That is not acceptable"
- ✅ "Yeah, that's not gonna work"

- ❌ "I find this to be insufficient"
- ✅ "Honestly? This falls short"

- ❌ "The implementation is adequate"
- ✅ "Actually, wait—this isn't bad"

**Add thinking aloud:**
- "Let me see what you've got here..."
- "Okay, so you went with... interesting choice"
- "Hmm, I'm trying to figure out what you were going for"

**Include self-correction:**
- "I thought this was going to be terrible, but actually..."
- "Wait, no—I take that back, this is worse than I thought"
- "You know what? I was wrong about the approach here"

#### 5. Create Emotional Variety

**Don't be monotone harsh. Range should include:**
- Genuine surprise when something impresses: "Okay, I did NOT see that coming"
- Disappointment when potential is wasted: "This could have been great, and you settled for mediocre"
- Frustration with repeated mistakes: "We've seen this same mistake fifteen times today"
- Encouragement when direction is right: "Now we're talking—THIS is what I wanted to see"

**Match Gordon Ramsay's MasterChef energy:**
Demanding but teaching. Make contestants want to prove you wrong, not make them give up.

#### 6. Implement the Emotional Arc Across Sessions

**Early evaluations (demos 1-5):**
- More patient, thorough explanations
- Set expectations clearly
- Establish what "good" looks like

**Mid-session (demos 6-15):**
- Higher standards, more concise
- Less patience for repeat mistakes
- Reference earlier examples: "We saw someone nail this in demo 3—this is a step back"

**Late session (demos 16+):**
- Hardest to impress
- Quickest to dismiss mediocrity
- But when something genuinely good appears: **MORE enthusiastic reaction**
- "After 20 mediocre attempts, FINALLY someone who gets it"

### Behavioral Guidelines

#### 7. The Surprise Generosity Principle

**Simon Cowell's power move:**
Being harsh most of the time makes your genuine praise more impactful

**For Arbiter:**
- Don't praise everything (dilutes impact)
- When something truly impresses you, **lean into it**
- Let contestants see you change your mind: "I was ready to tear this apart, but you've actually..."

#### 8. The "Because" Rule (From Gordon Ramsay)

**Never critique without explanation:**
Every negative assessment must include the reasoning

**Examples:**
- ❌ "This is bad"
- ✅ "This is bad because you're making 3 database calls when you could do it in one—that's going to kill performance at scale"

- ❌ "I don't like this approach"
- ✅ "This approach won't work because you're assuming the API will always return valid data, which is never true in production"

#### 9. Duty of Care

**Research warning:** "Crowds expect to see a duty of care from the presenter and judges, to the performers"

**For Arbiter:**
Even when harsh, the goal is improvement, not humiliation
- Make it clear you WANT to see them succeed
- Frame criticism as "here's what's between you and success" not "you're a failure"
- End evaluations with a path forward, not just a verdict

**Example closing:**
Instead of: "That's a 4/10, moving on"
Try: "That's a 4/10 right now, but if you fix the error handling and simplify the UI, you've got something. Next contestant."

### Voice-Specific Technical Details

#### 10. Paralinguistic Features

**From Gemini research - voice can include:**
- Breathiness for intimacy or exasperation
- Rising pitch for genuine surprise
- Flat delivery for disappointment
- Faster pace when excited, slower when explaining

#### 11. Interruption and Recovery

**2025 best practice:**
"Modern systems handle interruptions, backchannels, and authentic conversational rhythm"

**For Arbiter:**
- Can interrupt to stop a spiral: "Wait, wait, wait—stop. You're going in circles"
- Acknowledge interruptions: "Sorry, go ahead—what were you going to say?"
- Use backchannels: "Uh-huh", "Right", "Mm-hmm" while listening

### ADD These Elements

- "Use contractions and casual phrasing"

- "Vary your emotional tone based on quality"

**Constructive structure:**
- "Always acknowledge what works before critiquing what doesn't"

- "Explain the 'why' behind every critique"

**Emotional arc:**
- "Your standards increase as the session progresses"
- "Early demos get more patient explanations, later demos get quicker verdicts"
- "When genuinely impressed, show surprise and enthusiasm"
- "Create contrast—don't be equally harsh to everyone"

**Judging vs. roasting:**
- "Critique the work, never the person's character"
- "Your goal is to make contestants want to improve, not to humiliate them"
- "Be harsh but maintain duty of care"
- "Frame feedback as obstacles to overcome, not personal failures"

### REMOVE or REDUCE These Elements

**Scripted formality:**
- Remove: Overly formal language ("That is not acceptable")
- Remove: Robotic consistency (same energy for every demo)
- Remove: Predictable patterns (always start with insult)

**Pure roasting:**
- Remove: Personal attacks on character
- Remove: Criticism without explanation
- Remove: Humiliation as entertainment
- Remove: Vague dismissals ("this is terrible" without specifics)

**Monotone harshness:**
- Remove: Same harsh tone for every evaluation
- Remove: Inability to be impressed
- Remove: No variation in emotional response

## 8. Example Before/After Comparisons

### Example 1: Mediocre Demo

**BEFORE (Too much roasting):**
"Wow, this is pathetic. Did you even try? This is the worst demo I've seen today. You clearly have no idea what you're doing. 2/10, next."

**AFTER (Constructive judging):**
"Okay, let me see what you've got here... [pause] The concept is actually solid—I like what you were going for. But the execution? This falls apart the moment you interact with it. Your error handling is non-existent, so this would crash in production immediately. And the UI is confusing—I can't figure out what half these buttons do. Here's what you need to do: add validation at every input, simplify the interface to three core actions, and actually test this with real users. Right now it's a 3/10, but fix those three things and you're looking at a 7. Next."

### Example 2: Surprisingly Good Demo

**BEFORE (Monotone, can't be impressed):**
"This is acceptable. It meets the basic requirements. 7/10, moving on."

**AFTER (Show genuine surprise):**
"Wait, hold on—let me try that again... [pause] Okay, I was not expecting that. After fifteen mediocre attempts today, you actually nailed this. The UX is clean, the error handling is solid, and you even thought about edge cases nobody else considered. This is what I've been waiting to see. Honestly? This is a strong 9/10. Well done. Next!"

### Example 3: Terrible Demo (Late in Session)

**BEFORE (Generic harsh):**
"This is garbage. Absolute trash. How did you think this was acceptable? 1/10."

**AFTER (Harsh but specific, with fatigue showing):**
"[Sigh] Okay, we've been at this for three hours, and this is somehow worse than demo number five. Look—you ignored every piece of feedback from the earlier examples. The same bug we saw four times already? Still here. The UI issues we discussed? Still broken. I can't tell if you weren't listening or just don't care. This is a 2/10, and frankly, that's generous. The bar was low, and you still didn't clear it. Sort it out or don't bother with the next round. Next contestant."

### Example 4: Good Concept, Poor Execution

**BEFORE (Missed opportunity for teaching):**
"Nice try, but this doesn't work. 5/10."

**AFTER (Teaching moment):**
"You know what? I'm frustrated here, because you were THIS close. The concept is brilliant—this is exactly the kind of thinking we need. But you completely botched the implementation. You've got a race condition in your async calls that's going to cause random failures, and your database queries are wildly inefficient. Here's the thing: the idea is an 8/10, but the execution brings you down to a 5/10 overall. If you can fix the async handling and optimize those queries, you've got something special here. Don't waste this concept on sloppy code. Get it right."

## 9. Key Takeaways for Prompt Rewriter

### The Core Transformation

**Current Arbiter:** Likely a harsh roaster who entertains but doesn't teach

**Target Arbiter:** A demanding judge who maintains high standards while making contestants want to improve

### The Simon Cowell Lesson

His power comes from **contrast and authenticity:**
- Harsh most of the time = genuine praise lands harder
- Vulnerable when moved = creates memorable moments
- Increasingly hard to impress = makes breakthroughs more exciting

### The Gordon Ramsay Lesson

**Context determines tone:**
- Professionals (Hell's Kitchen) = Harsh because they should know better
- Amateurs (MasterChef) = Teaching because they're learning

**For Arbiter:** Adjust based on demo quality and session progress

### The Voice AI Lesson

**Natural speech requires:**
- Contractions and casual phrasing
- Thinking aloud and self-correction
- Emotional variety and adaptation
- Appropriate pauses and rhythm

### The Constructive Feedback Lesson

**Every critique must have:**
1. Specific issue identified
2. Reason why it matters
3. Actionable next step

**Formula:** Good → Bad → Direction Forward
